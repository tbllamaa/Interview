Volcanoes

Volcano eruptions between 2010-2018

VEI (Volcanic Explosivity Index) quantifies eruption power from 0 (non-explosive) to 8 (catastrophic) based on ejected material volume, aiding eruption classification and hazard assessment.

For elevation (in m), volcanic eruptions propel lava to varying heights influenced by eruption factors like magma composition and gas presence, aiding eruption analysis.

Different Types of Volcanoes

Different Types of Volcanoes:
Stratovolcano: Also known as composite volcanoes, these are tall, steep-sided mountains formed by alternating layers of lava flows, ash, and volcanic rocks. They can be highly explosive.
Shield Volcano: These are broad, gently sloping volcanoes created by numerous lava flows with low viscosity. They often have non-explosive eruptions.
Cinder Cone: Small, steep-sided volcanoes formed by ejected lava fragments during explosive eruptions. They have simple, conical shapes.
Lava Dome: These are created by the slow extrusion of highly viscous lava. They result in bulbous, dome-like features on a volcano.
Submarine Volcano: Situated under the ocean, these volcanoes erupt beneath the water's surface, potentially forming new islands when eruptions breach the sea.
Super Volcano: These are capable of catastrophic eruptions, ejecting enormous volumes of material. Yellowstone Caldera is an example.
Complex Volcano: A combination of features from different volcano types, resulting in complex, irregular shapes.
Fissure Volcano: Eruptions occur along elongated cracks or fissures in the Earth's crust, often producing extensive lava plateaus.

Tsunamis

Tsunamis between 2010-2018

Maximum water height during a tsunami, or tsunami wave height measures the highest seawater point reached during a tsunami event, crucial for effective warning systems and coastal safety planning

Earthquake magnitude,, often measured on the Richter scale, directly influences tsunami generation. Greater magnitudes result in more potent undersea disturbances and larger, potentially devastating tsunamis

Earthquakes

Earthquakes between 2010-2018

The focal depth of an earthquake, known as hypocenter depth indicates where the quake originates within the Earth's crust.

Earthquake magnitudeis a measure of seismic energy released during a quake. The Richter scale and moment magnitude scale quantify the earthquake's size and potential for damage.
